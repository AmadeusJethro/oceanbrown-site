What the PJM-MISO Seam Teaches

Validation failures, regime lessons, and five transferable conclusions from two years of ML spread-trading research
Author Owen Brown Domain Wholesale Power Markets • PJM / MISO Interface Development 70+ model versions • 2,500+ configurations tested
Abstract

The PJM-MISO electrical seam separates two of North America's largest grid operators. The real-time price spread at their interface, driven by regional generation mix and transmission constraints, is a natural candidate for systematic trading. Over approximately two years, this project developed ML models to predict the direction of that spread, iterating through more than 70 model versions and testing over ten algorithms. The initial results looked compelling. They were not.

A temporal alignment bug inflated the primary strategy's apparent win rate from approximately 52% to approximately 80% by feeding the model data from within the hour it was predicting. A switch from hub-based to interface-specific pricing restructured the signal space and invalidated all early results. Formal validation of the surviving model returned an hourly Sharpe of 0.015, a deflated Sharpe of 0.125, and a probability of backtest overfitting of 0.600. All three fail their standard thresholds.

This page is not a performance report. It is a case study in how edge stories collapse under their own audits, and what methodology survives once they do.

1. The Seam and the Opportunity

When power flows between PJM and MISO, it crosses a physical boundary where two different control areas, operating under different market rules and with different generation mixes, set prices independently. The Locational Marginal Price at that interface reflects congestion, transmission constraints, and the marginal cost of generation on each side. When those prices diverge, a spread exists. When the spread is predictable, it is, in principle, tradeable.

The seam spread has a natural structure that makes it an attractive research target. Both ISOs publish day-ahead prices around noon for the following operating day, creating a known information anchor. The spread exhibits short-term momentum: a large spread this hour makes a large spread next hour more likely than the unconditional average. Weather, seasonal demand, and fuel prices create patterns that repeat across years.

The question the project set out to answer was whether any of this structure was learnable with enough precision to clear transmission costs and survive out-of-sample. The answer, after two years, is: not by this approach, under this validation standard. The path to that conclusion is what is worth documenting.

2. The Measurement Decision That Changed Everything

Early models used regional hub prices: the PJM Western Hub and the MISO Indiana Hub. These are load-weighted averages across broad geographic areas, heavily traded, and available with decades of history. For seam trading specifically, they are the wrong price to model.

Hub prices aggregate prices from nodes spread across entire regions. The PJM Western Hub includes nodes in Ohio, Pennsylvania, and West Virginia. The MISO Indiana Hub spans the entire state. When a constraint fires at the actual PJM-MISO interface, it moves the interface price sharply. The hub price may barely register, diluted by nodes hundreds of miles from the seam.

When the project switched to interface-specific pricing nodes that capture the LMP at the physical points where power physically flows between the two systems, model accuracy improved by 1.4 percentage points from the data change alone, equivalent to the improvement that followed the first major algorithm upgrade.1 The interface-specific prices are noisier hour to hour but structurally cleaner for the specific question being asked.

Lesson: In any arbitrage research, the precision of the price you model determines the ceiling of the edge you can find. A regional average introduces structural noise that model complexity cannot compensate for, because the noise is correlated with the signal you are trying to learn.

The switch came with a cost: interface-specific data was available only from 2022 forward, shortening the usable window to roughly four years. Every pre-2022 result was invalidated by the measurement correction.

3. Seventy Iterations of Learning What Does Not Work

3.1 Algorithm Search

Over more than 70 model versions, the project evaluated more than ten algorithm families across 1,200+ hyperparameter configurations. The winner was a shallow gradient boosting classifier. The decisive factor was not sophistication but regularization: shallower trees, lower learning rates, and higher minimum leaf sizes generalized consistently better than any alternative.

Figure 1 — Algorithm Comparison: Relative Performance vs. GBM Baseline (Validation Period)
Relative performance of each algorithm family compared to the sklearn GradientBoostingClassifier baseline (set to 100%). All figures from validation-period evaluation, not holdout. Neural networks, ensemble stacking, and SVMs underperformed substantially. XGBoost and LightGBM were close but not competitive despite extensive tuning. Source: BOOK_08_EXPERIMENTS_FAILURES.2

XGBoost and LightGBM both received extensive hyperparameter tuning (243 and 120 combinations respectively) and still underperformed the sklearn implementation. The most likely explanation: with only 23 features and a small dataset, the regularization approach of the vanilla GBM matched the problem better than the more aggressive implementations optimized for large-scale data. XGBoost shines with 100K+ training samples; the seam dataset had roughly a quarter of that.2

Neural networks failed substantially: three different architectures, including a purpose-built LSTM for temporal patterns, all underperformed the GBM by 40-60%. The problem is not deep enough for deep learning: too few samples, relationships that are approximately linear, and a binary decision target that classification trees handle naturally.

3.2 Why Simple Features Won

The search for additional predictive signal was extensive. Transmission constraint data, load forecasts, wind generation, fuel mix, and area control error were all tested. None improved the model. Several hurt it.

Figure 2 — Autocorrelation Decay: Spread Predictability Horizon
Autocorrelation of the real-time spread vs. itself at increasing lags. Signal is meaningful at 1-hour lag but decays rapidly and approaches noise level by 48 hours. This explains why simple lagged features consistently outperformed models incorporating multi-day history or external signals. Source: BOOK_08_EXPERIMENTS_FAILURES.2

The rapid decay of autocorrelation explains most of the external-data failures. Transmission constraint data does not cause spread direction; it co-occurs with large spreads as a market outcome. The constraint is already priced into the LMP by the time it is observable. Load forecasts correlate with absolute price levels but not spreads. Area control error correlated at 0.09 with spread direction, essentially noise.

Figure 3 — Feature Complexity Penalty: Performance Change vs. Simple Baseline
Performance change relative to the 23-feature simple baseline when each addition or modification was tested. Every experiment that added complexity degraded performance. The FENOCH transmission constraint effort consumed six weeks of engineering time for an 8% loss. Source: BOOK_08_EXPERIMENTS_FAILURES.2
Lesson: Markets aggregate operational information into prices efficiently. External data sources that try to reconstruct what is already in the price (constraints, load, generation) add correlation noise rather than orthogonal signal. The surviving feature set was: lagged spreads, day-ahead spread, volatility, and time-of-day indicators.

4. The Bug That Explained Everything

The project's most consequential failure was discovered not by the project's own audit but by an external review asking a question no internal check had explicitly posed.

One strategy variant added partial-hour features: five-minute price data from the first 25-30 minutes of each hour. The idea was sound: use early intra-hour price action to refine the prediction for that hour. The implementation contained a one-hour temporal misalignment. The code shifted partial-hour data by +1 hour before merging with hourly records. Due to the hour-ending timestamp convention in the underlying dataset, that shift aligned partial-hour observations from the current hour with the hourly record for that same hour, rather than with the next hour's record.

The model was not predicting. It was measuring. With access to 25 minutes of a 60-minute window, it could see roughly 42% of the data that determined the outcome. Feature importance analysis showed that 59.2% of predictive weight concentrated in the leaked partial-hour features.3

The internal audit had verified all of the standard checklist items: no negative shifts, no shuffle, scalers fit on training data only, thresholds selected from validation data only. The checklist did not verify that the shift amount was correct for the timestamp convention in use. Verifying that a shift exists is not the same as verifying that it produces the right temporal relationship.

The external review caught it by asking: what hour are you actually submitting for? That question surfaced the misalignment immediately. An empirical check confirmed it: the five-minute average for 9:00-10:00 exactly matched the hourly record labeled 10:00, confirming the hour-ending convention and the one-hour shift error.

Lesson: High apparent efficiency should be a warning, not confirmation. A model that captures a large fraction of theoretical maximum in an efficient market is likely measuring information it should not have access to. The correct response to a 80% win rate in a seam-trading strategy is skepticism, not deployment.

5. The Formal Verdict

After correcting the lookahead bug, formal validation was applied to the best surviving model using combinatorially symmetric cross-validation (CSCV), deflated Sharpe ratio (DSR), and probability of backtest overfitting (PBO) analysis. These tests are frequency-independent: unlike standard train/test splits, they explicitly account for the number of configurations tested during development.

Figure 4 — Validation Scorecard: Actual vs. Threshold
Three validation metrics, each plotted against its standard threshold. All three fail. Sharpe (hourly) reaches 2.1% of the minimum standard. DSR reaches 13.9%. PBO exceeds its ceiling by a factor of 12, shown here as its inverse (1 − PBO) vs. the 0.95 minimum. Source: formal CSCV/DSR/PBO validation artifact.4

Sharpe ratio (hourly): 0.015. This sits at roughly 2% of the minimum standard of 0.70. Even accounting for structural compression of hourly returns relative to daily Sharpe, this is near-zero signal-to-noise.

Deflated Sharpe ratio: 0.125. The DSR adjusts the observed Sharpe for the number of configurations tested during development. A DSR below 0.90 indicates the observed Sharpe is likely an artifact of selection across configurations rather than a stable signal. At 0.125, the selection-artifact explanation is the more probable one.

Probability of backtest overfitting: 0.600. In 60% of combinatorial subsample tests, the configuration that ranked best in-sample did not rank best out-of-sample. The selection process shows strong systematic evidence of overfitting. P&L variance across CPCV folds exceeds the mean fold result by approximately 50×, confirming that performance is not stable across temporal subsamples.4

Stress-window analysis adds a regime dimension. Performance was highly concentrated in a single favorable year; the adjacent year showed a loss of comparable magnitude. This is not just a model failure. Spread opportunities at the seam cluster around weather events, grid stress, and seasonal demand patterns that do not repeat uniformly year to year. A model that captured a single favorable year is not the same as a model that captures the structural opportunity.

6. What This Page Does Not Claim

This page does not claim profitable trading performance in any validated configuration. It does not claim the PJM-MISO seam is untradeable by other approaches, with different data, or under different market conditions. It does not claim the legacy performance figures from pre-validation phases were accurate. It does not provide dollar-denominated return figures of any kind.

The position is narrow: this specific project, under this specific validation protocol, with this specific approach, did not demonstrate a tradeable edge. What it demonstrated is a replicable methodology for finding that out before it matters.

7. Five Transferable Conclusions

The results are negative. The lessons are not.

1. Verify temporal alignment empirically, not structurally. The standard checklist (no negative shifts, no shuffle, train-only scalers) is necessary but not sufficient. The correct verification is empirical: extract the feature value at time T, then verify manually that it could have been known at time T without access to T's outcome. The bug described here would have been caught in five minutes by this check.

2. Price the thing you are trading, not a proxy for it. Hub prices are the wrong measurement for seam arbitrage. Interface-specific LMPs, despite shorter history, are structurally cleaner for this question. The measurement decision dominates the model decision.

3. PBO and DSR are not optional post-hoc checks. A standard train/test split on time-series data, even properly implemented with no lookahead, cannot detect the selection bias introduced by testing 2,500+ configurations. PBO makes that selection pressure explicit. A PBO above 0.50 is not a nuance; it means the strategy selection process was not working.

4. When features stop helping, stop adding them. Every external data source tested added noise. Transmission constraints, load forecasts, weather, fuel mix, area control error: none produced marginal information value that cleared their complexity cost. The signal was in the price itself. This is consistent with what efficient-market theory predicts for publicly available operational data.

5. Regime concentration masquerades as edge. A model that performs well in one out of four stress windows has not found a persistent signal; it has captured a regime. Walk-forward monthly retraining mitigates but does not solve this. The only real solution is a longer out-of-sample window with explicit regime labeling.
