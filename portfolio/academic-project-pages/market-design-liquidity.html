Project 01

Market Design, Liquidity, and the Illusion of Edge in Event Markets

A research narrative on what prediction markets taught me about execution realism, false diversification, venue portability, and why extraordinary backtests often disappear under stricter validation.

Primary Theme Market microstructure and execution
Empirical Basis Tens of millions of historical trades across multiple venue-specific datasets
Portfolio Use Research case study / methods paper style

Structured Abstract

Question. When an event-market strategy looks profitable, how much of that apparent edge survives once chronology, liquidity, costs, and cross-strategy correlation are handled honestly?

Approach. I combined exploratory strategy research, liquidity studies, leakage audits, and portfolio correlation analysis across Polymarket- and Kalshi-related datasets.

Main finding. The strongest lesson was not that prediction markets were easy to beat; it was that the edge story changed dramatically once I enforced train/test separation, modeled fillability, and treated correlation clusters as real risk.

Why it matters. This is directly analogous to congestion and FTR trading, where theoretical alpha is meaningless unless it survives market design, execution, and regime-dependence.

Research Problem

Why the project mattered

Prediction markets are a useful laboratory for thinking about microeconomics. They are small, noisy, segmented, and heavily shaped by rule design. That makes them a good place to study the same kinds of questions that appear in power trading: where does apparent edge come from, how much of it is structural, and how much disappears once costs and operational constraints are treated seriously?

The goal of this work was not simply to find profitable heuristics. It was to understand which findings were robust, which were artifacts of data or execution assumptions, and what those distinctions imply for trading systems more broadly.

Core Findings

1. Chronological leakage can manufacture spectacular but meaningless performance

One of the most important results was negative: a leakage audit showed that a strategy claiming to turn $1,000 into $877,974 had trained and tested on the same 2025 data slice. That rendered the backtest invalid. The value of this result is methodological. In thin, regime-sensitive markets, extraordinary performance is often a warning sign rather than proof of alpha.

Methodological takeaway Any serious event-market research framework must enforce strict chronological splits, not random sampling and not same-period calibration. This is exactly the same discipline required in congestion or transmission trading, where model selection against the delivery period can quietly leak future structure.

2. Fillability is strongly asymmetric across price regions

A dedicated liquidity study showed a sharp difference between cheap YES underdogs and expensive NO favorites. In one large Polymarket snapshot, YES underdogs priced at 1-5 cents had an average trade size of only $17.41, and just 8,114 of 1.56 million trades were $500+ fills. By contrast, NO favorites at 95+ cents had an average trade size of $357.79, with roughly 1.25 million $500+ fills out of 15.55 million trades.

Region Average trade size $500+ fill frequency Interpretation
YES underdogs (1-5c) $17.41 0.52% Interesting on paper, difficult at size
NO favorites (95+c) $357.79 8.0% Far more executable for institutional sizing
Microeconomic interpretation The same expected-value signal can be very different economically depending on who is willing to trade, how much size clears, and where inventory risk sits in the book. In other words: theoretical edge and executable edge are not the same object.

3. Diversification can be fake when latent factors dominate

Correlation analysis across strategy clusters showed that many apparently independent crypto strategies were in fact one common-factor bet. On August 30, 2024, seventeen crypto strategies failed simultaneously. The lesson was not only about crypto; it was about portfolio construction. A set of strategies built from superficially different tickers, thresholds, or windows may still collapse together when the same hidden driver moves. 

Loss cluster example
- 17 crypto strategies failed on the same date
- Multiple assets, bands, and time windows moved together
- Apparent diversification was mostly cosmetic
Clustered losses mattered more than average win rates. This is conceptually close to path correlation in congestion books or weather-linked common-mode risk across FTR exposures.

4. Strategy portability across venues is not automatic

Cross-venue work on Kalshi and Polymarket suggested that identical-looking ideas could behave very differently once fee schedules, contract structure, category composition, and user mix changed. The practical consequence is that a strategy should be assumed venue-specific until re-estimated under that venue's own market design.

This is the same principle that applies when moving from one ISO or product structure to another: rules, liquidity pools, participant composition, and settlement design all change the feasible opportunity set.

How I Would Frame the Contribution

Academic thesis

The main contribution is not a specific trading rule. It is a compact empirical argument: event-market edge is a joint function of validation discipline, venue design, and execution constraints. Once those are enforced, the number of believable opportunities shrinks sharply, but the surviving conclusions become much more useful.

Portfolio framing I would present this as a methods-heavy project on market design and execution realism, not as a brag sheet of backtested returns. The interesting part is the distinction between statistical signal and economically realizable signal.

Relevance to FTR and Congestion Trading

  • Execution realism: just as with thin event-market books, nominal alpha in congestion or FTRs can disappear once liquidity, queue position, and fill assumptions are modeled honestly.
  • Rule-dependent edge: venue design in prediction markets plays the same role that auction rules, product definitions, and settlement mechanics play in power markets.
  • Correlation clustering: multiple apparent strategies may collapse into one latent risk factor under stressed regimes.
  • Chronological validation: strategy research must respect time, regime boundaries, and the impossibility of learning from the future.
