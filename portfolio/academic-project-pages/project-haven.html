Project 02

Project Haven: A Fee-, Slippage-, and Correlation-Aware Selection Engine for Same-Day Event Markets

A research and systems project on how to monitor, rank, and shadow-trade same-day prediction-market opportunities without pretending that costs, execution, or diversification are someone else's problem.

Primary Theme Selection and execution design
Operational Model 24-hour monitoring, final 4-hour action window
Portfolio Use Productized quant systems case study

Structured Abstract

Question. If small mispricings persist in same-day event markets, what is the right way to act on them without destroying expected value through fees, slippage, or correlated risk?

Approach. I designed a system that monitors all markets across a rolling day, narrows attention to a short action window, computes fee-adjusted expected value, and produces explainable ranked candidates for human review or shadow trading.

Main finding. The central design lesson was that a viable market-selection system must be explicit about its economics: break-even probability, cost sensitivity, depth, and correlation cannot be afterthoughts.

Why it matters. This turns a vague trading thesis into an operational research problem, which is much closer to how serious energy or quant workflows are actually managed.

Research Problem

From trading idea to selection system

Project Haven began from a simple observation: if a favorite-longshot bias exists, the economically relevant question is not merely whether prices are biased, but whether the bias survives costs, liquidity constraints, and portfolio interaction. That pushes the problem away from "build a bot" and toward "design a decision system for selecting scarce, executable opportunities."

The resulting architecture treats market participation as a constrained optimization problem. The system first watches all same-day markets, builds telemetry, and only enters an action mode in the final hours before resolution when the relationship between price, depth, and realized uncertainty is most stable.

Operational Model

Two-stage workflow

Window Objective Function
T-24h to T-4h Monitoring and telemetry Track price, spread, book depth, stability, and market quality without acting.
T-4h to T Selection and ranking Apply filters, compute fee-adjusted EV, generate ranked Top-K candidates, and send them to a human-readable dashboard.
Design choice The two-window model is the main point. It acknowledges that observation and execution are distinct tasks and that the right information structure for monitoring is not the right one for taking risk.

Economic Logic

Make the math visible

Haven was designed so that every ranked candidate could be justified economically. The project specification explicitly surfaces fee formulas, break-even probabilities, and expected-value calculations rather than hiding them behind a score.

Fee: f = 0.07 × P × (1 − P)
Break-even probability: q* = (P + f) / (1 − 2f)
Expected value: EV = q(1 − P − f) + (1 − q)(−P − f)

This matters because in small-edge systems, one cent of slippage or one poor assumption about break-even can erase the entire thesis. The system therefore prefers explainability over score opacity.

Selection Logic

What the system actually ranked

  • time to close and whether the contract is inside the action window
  • favorite-side price bands and fallback bands
  • fee-adjusted expected value and margin over break-even
  • book depth and estimated slippage at target size
  • theme caps and per-underlying caps to prevent hidden concentration
  • rule clarity and market quality filters for ambiguous or parlay-like contracts

In other words, Haven was built as a disciplined ranking engine. It is less about forecasting everything than about refusing to select candidates unless they satisfy operational, microstructural, and diversification requirements simultaneously.

Main Systems Insight

The right output is not a trade, but a justified decision packet

One of the most useful design conclusions was that a production-oriented selection system should emit a compact operator packet rather than a raw trade signal: Top-K ranked candidates, exclusion reasons, order book snapshots, configuration hash, and enough math for a human to review the trade thesis. That is much closer to a real desk workflow than an opaque auto-execution rule. 

Haven output artifact
- ranked candidates
- excluded markets with reason codes
- fee and break-even math
- order book snapshots
- configuration version / run metadata
The system was intentionally designed around auditability and reviewability rather than black-box automation.

Why This Is Relevant Beyond Prediction Markets

Connection to energy and quant workflows

  • Candidate ranking under scarce capital: very similar to selecting which spreads, paths, or congestion situations deserve attention.
  • Cost-aware decision making: edge must clear fees, slippage, and operational friction before it is real.
  • Correlation caps: category caps in Haven play the same role as concentration limits in power books.
  • Human-in-the-loop workflow: the system was built to support operator review, which is exactly how many real trading and dispatch workflows work.
