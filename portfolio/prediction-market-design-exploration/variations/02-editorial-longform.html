Market Design & Liquidity in Event Markets

Prediction markets promise efficient aggregation of dispersed beliefs into prices, but their microstructure remains poorly understood compared to traditional financial markets. This project investigates how automated market maker design choices—cost function parameterization, fee structures, and liquidity provision—affect price discovery quality in binary event markets.1

The central empirical contribution compares logarithmic market scoring rules against continuous limit order books using synchronized orderbook data from Polymarket and Kalshi. LMSR exhibits superior price stability in thin markets but incurs substantially higher inventory costs during rapid information arrival events.

Adaptive-spread LMSR reduces adverse selection costs by 34% compared to fixed-spread parameterization.

A liquidity-sensitive fee structure is proposed that adapts bid-ask spreads dynamically based on realized volatility and order flow imbalance, borrowing from the market microstructure literature on optimal market making under inventory constraints.

Methodology

Simulation experiments calibrate LMSR and CLOB models to observed market parameters, then subject both to synthetic information shocks of varying magnitude. Empirical validation uses tick-level data from WebSocket feeds captured by the data logging infrastructure described in Project III.

Haven Selection & Shadow-Trading System

Haven selection identifies prediction market contracts where prices persistently deviate from fair value due to structural factors: low liquidity, retail sentiment bias, or hedging demand from correlated positions. The shadow-trading system executes systematically against these mispricings using a relative-value framework.

The selection algorithm scores contracts on four dimensions: pricing efficiency, liquidity depth, mean-reversion tendency, and correlation to existing portfolio exposures. Contracts passing all thresholds enter a shadow book where positions are sized using a modified Kelly criterion constrained by binary payoff structure.2

Reliable Market Data Loggers

Research-quality prediction market analysis requires continuous, gap-free data capture across exchanges with heterogeneous API paradigms. This infrastructure project builds fault-tolerant logging systems with automatic gap detection, backfill capability, and schema versioning for long-term data integrity.

The architecture uses a checkpoint-recovery pattern: each logger maintains a write-ahead log, persists to Parquet with configurable partitioning, and registers heartbeats with a supervisor. On failure, the supervisor restarts from the last checkpoint and initiates REST-based backfill to close gaps. Current deployment covers three exchanges with 99.94% uptime over 180 days.
